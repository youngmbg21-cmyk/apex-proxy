How to read any report
01
Verdict Banner
The coloured badge at the top (BUY / HOLD / SELL) is the AI's bottom-line conclusion. Green = bullish, Amber = neutral, Red = bearish. Always read the one-line detail next to it for context.
02
KPI Row
Six key metrics specific to that module. Green values are positive signals, red are warnings, amber are neutral or mixed. These give you a quick at-a-glance summary before reading the full analysis.
03
Sections & Tables
The detailed breakdown. Each section has a numbered title. Tables compare metrics side-by-side. Bullet points highlight the most important takeaways. Read top to bottom — they are ordered by priority.
⚡ Full Intelligence Report
The Full Intelligence Report runs all 7 modules simultaneously on a single ticker and produces a unified document. At the top you'll find a Master Consensus Summary — a tally of how many modules returned bullish, neutral, or bearish verdicts, giving you an overall signal that no single module can provide alone. Below it, each module's full report is presented as a numbered chapter. Use the ↺ Retry button on any failed chapter to re-run just that module without restarting the whole report. This is the most powerful way to research a stock — running it before any major investment decision is strongly recommended.

Goldman Sachs Stock Screener
Goldman Sachs · Senior Equity Analyst
Stock Fundamental
▼
What it does

This module acts as a Goldman Sachs-style equity analyst conducting a full fundamental screen on a stock. It evaluates whether a stock is worth buying given your risk tolerance and time horizon. It weighs valuation metrics against sector averages, builds bull and bear scenarios with price targets, and ends with specific trade parameters — entry zone, stop loss, and target price.

Key outputs to look for
P/E Ratio vs Sector
Is the stock cheap or expensive relative to peers? Below sector average = potentially undervalued.
Revenue Growth
Accelerating growth is a bullish signal. Decelerating growth is a warning, even if still positive.
Bull / Bear / Base Cases
Three price targets with probabilities. Focus on the risk/reward ratio — is the upside larger than the downside?
Trade Parameters
Entry zone, stop loss, and target price. Use these as a starting point for your own position sizing.
FCF Yield
Free cash flow yield above 3% generally indicates a healthy, cash-generative business.
Target Price
12-month analyst consensus price target. Compare to current price to gauge implied upside.
When to use it
  • You've heard about a stock and want a quick, structured opinion before researching further
  • You want to compare a stock against its sector to see if it's cheap or expensive
  • You want specific entry/exit levels before opening a position
  • First step in any new stock research process — run this before all others
How to interpret the verdict
VerdictWhat it means
STRONG BUYValuation is attractive, fundamentals are strong, risk/reward is highly favourable. High conviction.
BUYGood risk/reward, solid fundamentals. Worth buying at the right entry price.
HOLDFairly valued or mixed signals. Keep existing positions but don't add aggressively.
SELL / AVOIDOvervalued, deteriorating fundamentals, or risk outweighs reward. Step aside.

Morgan Stanley DCF Valuation
Morgan Stanley · VP Investment Banking
Stock Valuation Model
▼
What it does

A Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model projects a company's future free cash flows and discounts them back to today's value using a cost of capital (WACC). The result is an intrinsic value — what the stock is theoretically worth based purely on its expected cash generation. If intrinsic value is above the current price, the stock is undervalued. This is the gold standard of fundamental valuation used by M&A bankers and long-term investors.

Key outputs to look for
Intrinsic Value vs Price
The most important number. A large gap between intrinsic value and market price = potential opportunity or overvaluation.
Margin of Safety
How much buffer exists if assumptions prove too optimistic. Warren Buffett looks for 25–30%+ margin of safety.
WACC
The discount rate. Higher WACC = lower intrinsic value. Check that assumptions are reasonable (typically 8–12%).
Sensitivity Table
Shows intrinsic value under different WACC and growth assumptions. Look for how robust the valuation is across scenarios.
Terminal Growth Rate
The long-term growth rate assumed beyond 5 years. Should be close to GDP growth (2–3%). Beware of inflated assumptions.
FCF Projection
Five-year free cash flow forecast. Look for consistent growth and expanding margins as signs of a quality business.
When to use it
  • You want to know what a stock is truly worth, not just what the market is paying today
  • Long-term investors (5yr+) — short-term traders should rely more on technical analysis
  • Before a large position — use as a sanity check on valuation
  • Pair with the Goldman Screener: if both agree it's undervalued, conviction is much higher
Important caveat

DCF models are highly sensitive to assumptions. Small changes in WACC or growth rates create large swings in intrinsic value. Always use the sensitivity table to understand the range of outcomes, not just the base case number. Treat the result as a range, not a precise figure.

Bridgewater Risk Assessment
Bridgewater Associates · Ray Dalio Principles
Portfolio Risk
▼
What it does

Inspired by Bridgewater's All Weather framework, this module stress-tests a portfolio against real-world scenarios — market crashes, rate spikes, sector selloffs, and macro shocks. It analyses correlation between holdings (do they all move together?), concentration risk, and estimates drawdowns under different conditions. It then recommends specific hedges and rebalancing actions to reduce risk.

Key outputs to look for
Risk Score (1–10)
Overall portfolio risk. Below 4 = conservative. 5–7 = moderate. Above 7 = aggressive/concentrated. Neither is inherently wrong.
Max Drawdown Estimate
The worst expected loss in a severe bear market. Use this to gut-check: could you stomach losing this much without panic selling?
Stress Test Scenarios
How the portfolio performs in specific crises. The dollar impact column is the most useful — it makes abstract percentages real.
Correlation Analysis
High correlation between holdings means less true diversification. Ideally your portfolio has a mix of low-correlation assets.
Concentration Risk
If one sector or stock is over 30–40% of your portfolio, concentration risk is high. This is a key warning to watch.
Hedge Recommendations
Specific ETFs or instruments suggested to reduce downside risk. Gold (GLD), bonds (BND), and inverse ETFs are common suggestions.
When to use it
  • After building or changing your portfolio — always run a risk check after any major rebalancing
  • During volatile markets — understand your true exposure before the market moves against you
  • If you feel anxious about your portfolio — the stress tests help you understand worst-case scenarios so you can plan rationally
  • Annually as a portfolio health check — are your risk levels still aligned with your goals?

JPMorgan Earnings Analyzer
JPMorgan · Senior Equity Research Analyst
Stock Earnings
▼
What it does

This module focuses specifically on the upcoming earnings event — the single most volatile moment for any stock. It analyses the company's historical beat rate, estimates the implied post-earnings move (from options pricing), and maps out bull/bear/base scenarios for what happens after the print. Earnings surprises routinely move stocks 8–20%, making this one of the highest-impact modules for active investors and traders.

Key outputs to look for
Beat Rate
What % of past quarters did this company beat estimates? Above 70% = historically reliable outperformer. Below 50% = risky.
Implied Move (±%)
How much the options market expects the stock to move after earnings. Useful for deciding whether to hold through the report.
EPS & Revenue Estimates
The consensus targets the company must beat. Missing either usually causes a sell-off even if the other metric beats.
Bull / Bear Scenarios
Price impact ranges for different outcomes. Use these to set realistic expectations and plan your reaction before the number hits.
Key Metrics to Watch
The specific line items Wall Street is focused on beyond EPS — guidance, margins, and segment revenues matter as much as the headline number.
Trading Recommendation
Hold through, sell before, or buy the dip after? This section gives a specific stance with reasoning.
When to use it
  • In the 1–2 weeks before a company reports earnings — this is its primary use case
  • You own the stock and need to decide whether to hold through the print or reduce risk first
  • You're considering buying a stock and want to know if earnings could create a better entry point
  • Understanding which specific metrics the market is watching — guidance often matters more than EPS

Citadel Technical Analysis
Citadel · Senior Quantitative Trader
Stock Technical
▼
What it does

Technical analysis studies price patterns, moving averages, momentum indicators, and volume to identify entry and exit points. Unlike fundamental analysis which asks "is this stock worth buying?", technical analysis asks "is right now the right time to buy it?". This module uses a Citadel-style quantitative framework combining multiple indicators — RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands, volume — to produce a concrete trade plan with exact entry zones, stop losses, and targets.

Key outputs to look for
Trend Direction
Is the stock in an uptrend, downtrend, or sideways? Trading with the trend dramatically improves win rates.
RSI (14)
Below 30 = oversold (potential buy). Above 70 = overbought (potential sell). Between 40–60 = neutral momentum.
vs 200-Day MA
The most important long-term signal. Price above 200-DMA = bull market structure. Below = bear market structure.
Support & Resistance
Price levels where buyers/sellers cluster. Buying near support and selling near resistance is the core of technical trading.
Risk/Reward Ratio
Distance to target vs distance to stop. Aim for at least 2:1 — meaning potential gain is twice the potential loss.
MACD Signal
A bullish MACD crossover confirms upward momentum. A bearish crossover warns of weakening price action.
When to use it
  • You've decided to buy a stock (from fundamentals) and want the optimal entry point
  • Short-term traders and swing traders — this is your primary module
  • You're already in a position and want to know if you should hold, add, or reduce
  • Pair with the Goldman Screener: fundamentals say buy, technicals say the chart is breaking out = high conviction trade

Renaissance Pattern Finder
Renaissance Technologies · Quantitative Researcher
Quantitative Statistical
▼
What it does

Renaissance Technologies is the most successful quant hedge fund in history, known for finding statistical patterns invisible to human analysts. This module applies a similar framework — analysing seasonal patterns, calendar effects, institutional ownership changes, short interest, and insider activity to identify repeatable statistical edges. It surfaces the top 3 tradeable patterns with historical win rates and average returns.

Key outputs to look for
Win Rate
What % of the time has this pattern produced a profit historically? Above 60% is considered a strong statistical edge.
Seasonal Patterns
Some stocks reliably rise or fall at specific times of year (pre-earnings run, January effect, end of quarter). These are actionable calendars.
Short Interest
High short interest + positive catalyst = potential short squeeze, amplifying gains. Low short interest = more stable price action.
Institutional Accumulation
When large funds are buying (increasing ownership), it creates sustained buying pressure. "Follow the smart money" is a core quant signal.
Insider Buying
Company executives buying their own stock is one of the most bullish signals possible. They only buy when they believe the stock is undervalued.
Put/Call Ratio
Below 0.7 = bullish options positioning. Above 1.2 = bearish hedging. Extreme readings often precede reversals.
When to use it
  • You want to time your entry — not just buy, but buy at the statistically optimal moment
  • Understanding the "invisible" forces driving a stock beyond price and fundamentals
  • Checking if institutional money is accumulating or distributing — follow the smart money
  • Most useful on well-known, liquid stocks with long price histories (AAPL, NVDA, TSLA etc.)

McKinsey Macro Economic Impact
McKinsey Global Institute · Sovereign Wealth Advisor
Macro Portfolio
▼
What it does

Macro analysis looks at the big picture — interest rates, inflation, GDP growth, dollar strength, and geopolitical risk — and translates those forces into specific portfolio implications. Most retail investors focus on individual stocks while ignoring the macro tide that can lift or sink everything at once. This module bridges that gap, analysing how the current macro regime affects your specific holdings and what to do about it.

Key outputs to look for
Macro Regime
Early Cycle, Mid Cycle, Late Cycle, or Recession. Each regime historically favours different sectors. Late Cycle = rotate to defensive stocks.
Fed Policy Impact
Rising rates hurt growth stocks and bonds. Falling rates boost them. This is the single biggest macro driver of equity valuations.
Recession Risk %
Estimated probability of recession in the next 12 months. Above 40% warrants defensive positioning. Above 60% = consider significant risk reduction.
Sector Impact Table
How your specific concern (inflation, rates, geopolitics) hits each sector. Use this to decide where to overweight and underweight.
6-Month Action Plan
Specific ETFs and rebalancing moves recommended for the current macro environment. These are actionable, not just theoretical.
Tail Risk Hedge
A specific recommendation for protecting against low-probability, high-impact events. Gold, TLT, UVXY, or cash are common suggestions.
When to use it
  • When macro news is dominating markets — Fed decisions, CPI prints, jobs reports
  • Quarterly portfolio review — is the macro regime shifting and are your holdings aligned?
  • You feel uncertain about the market direction and want a structured framework to think through it
  • Before making large portfolio changes — understand the macro tailwinds and headwinds first
